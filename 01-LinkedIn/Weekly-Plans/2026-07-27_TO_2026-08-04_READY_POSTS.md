### Series
Marketing Beyond Marketing

### Topic
Why B2B results cannot be demanded overnight

### Draft
“When will the leads start converting?”

That question captures one of the hardest tensions in B2B marketing.

It is a fair question.

Marketing should be accountable for pipeline and business outcomes.

But B2B buying rarely happens the moment someone sees an advertisement, downloads a report, or attends a webinar.

The buyer may still need to:

- understand the problem,
- trust the solution,
- convince internal stakeholders,
- secure a budget,
- compare vendors,
- and reduce the risk of making the wrong decision.

That process takes time.

The real challenge is balancing two truths:

Marketing must create momentum quickly.

But trust and revenue do not always move at the same speed.

A long sales cycle should never become an excuse for weak marketing.

At the same time, expecting an enterprise buyer to make a high-risk decision overnight is not realistic either.

Good B2B marketing does not only generate leads.

It keeps building confidence until the buyer is ready to act.

Where do you think the line sits between giving a B2B buyer time and holding marketing accountable?

### Series
Build With Vikash

### Topic
The 2017 decision that moved me from offline marketing into digital

### Draft
In 2017, I was still not fully convinced that digital marketing would become as important as everyone claimed.

A large number of marketers were moving toward digital.

I kept wondering:

How many people really use the internet?

How many people own smartphones?

Was this change actually as big as people said it was?

Then I began noticing what companies such as Lava and other handset manufacturers were doing.

Smartphones were becoming more affordable.

That was the moment it clicked for me.

The biggest barrier was beginning to disappear.

If access became affordable, customer behaviour could change much faster than most businesses expected.

I realised that if I did not start learning then, I might spend the next few years trying to catch up.

So I began spending time with the digital team.

I started small.

I learned something every day.

Over time, digital marketing became part of my work rather than something happening in another department.

Today, I can work on the idea, help create a website or application, and then take it to market.

I still do not believe offline and digital marketing are enemies.

Both are tools.

The real skill is recognising when customer behaviour is changing—and learning before the shift becomes obvious to everyone.

## Wednesday, 29 July 2026

### Series
AGMP With Vikash

### Topic
Diagnosing the real business problem instead of treating symptoms

### Draft
AGMP has changed the way I listen to business problems.

A person may say:

“My body hurts.”

“I cannot sleep.”

“I feel weak.”

Those are real problems.

But they may only be symptoms of an underlying condition.

Business conversations work in a similar way.

A team may say:

“Sales are declining.”

“The leads are poor.”

“Employees are not performing.”

“Customers are complaining.”

The first statement is not always the real problem.

The underlying cause may be pricing, positioning, capacity, incentives, process design, organisational structure, or a poor customer experience.

Earlier, I was more likely to start solving the issue exactly as it was described.

Now I try to pause and ask:

What is producing this symptom?

What information is missing?

Who sees the problem differently?

What would have to be true for this explanation to be correct?

A manager’s job is not only to respond quickly.

It is to make sure the organisation is solving the right problem.

## Thursday, 30 July 2026

### Series
Buyer Psychology With Vikash

### Topic
What repeated free-trial accounts taught me about SaaS pricing

### Draft
Some users kept creating new email addresses to continue using our SaaS product without paying for it.

At first, it looked like simple free-trial misuse.

But their behaviour was also telling us something important.

The need was real.

They wanted the product.

They were willing to spend time creating new accounts to continue using it.

What they were not willing—or perhaps able—to do was pay the price we had set.

That experience taught me that customer need may remain similar across markets, but willingness and ability to pay do not.

The same product can face very different realities depending on:

- purchasing power,
- local alternatives,
- perceived value,
- buying habits,
- and the cost of switching.

It also reminded me that pricing is not only a finance decision.

It is part of product strategy, market entry, customer psychology, and growth.

The answer is not always to reduce the price.

Sometimes the business needs a different package, a usage-based model, stronger paid differentiation, or tighter trial controls.

Customer behaviour often reveals what a survey will not.

The mistake is judging the behaviour before understanding what it is trying to tell us.

Why I started building QueryCite

### Draft
I started building QueryCite because I could not answer a simple question about my own personal brand:

Why was my name not appearing in AI-generated answers?

I began trying to understand how businesses and individuals become discoverable and trustworthy to large language models.

The deeper I went, the more uncertain the field appeared.

AI visibility is still developing.

I found useful advice, but I could not find an affordable, practical starting point for a small business or an individual professional.

I could also see why a business might hesitate to invest heavily in a field it is still trying to understand.

That creates a difficult gap.

Businesses may want to understand AI visibility without being ready for a large consulting engagement.

So I decided to build an affordable starting point.

QueryCite is my attempt to help a user explore a practical question:

Can AI systems discover, understand, and trust my business or personal brand?

I am not building it because I believe SEO is dead.

I am building it because customer discovery is expanding beyond traditional search, and businesses need a sensible way to start preparing.

The product is still evolving.

So am I.

That is exactly why I am documenting the journey.

## Saturday, 1 August 2026

### Series
Mobility With Vikash

### Topic
The child-seat request that exposed an overlooked customer need

### Draft
A customer recently asked us for something I had never seen in a regular cab booking in India:

A child seat for their one-year-old.

The parents had booked a cab for local sightseeing.

At first, the request sounded unusual because it is not a standard option in the bookings I usually see.

Instead of treating it as a difficult request, I called them to understand why it mattered.

They explained that they could carry the child in their lap.

They could also let the child sleep on the seat.

But neither option felt completely safe or comfortable.

They would remain worried about the child falling.

And carrying a one-year-old throughout a long sightseeing journey is not easy either.

That conversation changed the way I looked at the request.

They were not asking for luxury.

They were asking for safety, comfort, and peace of mind.

I raised the point with our management so we could evaluate whether it might be possible.

Customer-experience innovation does not always begin with a major strategy meeting.

Sometimes it begins with one request that initially sounds unusual.

The job is to listen long enough to understand the need behind it.

## Monday, 3 August 2026

### Series
Marketing Beyond Marketing

### Topic
The biggest competitor in B2B is often no decision

### Draft
In B2B, your biggest competitor is not always another company.

Very often, it is no decision.

A buyer may agree that the problem is real.

They may like your solution.

They may even believe your price is reasonable.

And still do nothing.

Why?

Because changing a vendor, introducing new software, or redesigning a process creates risk.

Someone has to justify the decision.

Someone has to manage implementation.

Someone may be blamed if it fails.

Doing nothing can feel safer than making an imperfect choice.

That is why B2B marketing cannot stop at proving that the product is better.

It must also reduce the perceived risk of change.

This can happen through:

- clear implementation plans,
- evidence from similar customers,
- realistic expectations,
- internal business cases,
- stakeholder-specific communication,
- and confidence that the vendor will remain accountable after the sale.

The buyer is not only asking:

“Is this solution good?”

They are also asking:

“Will choosing it create a problem for me?”

The strongest B2B brands answer both questions.

#MarketingBeyondMarketing #B2BBuying #DemandGeneration

### Series
Build With Vikash

### Topic
From using marketing tools to building them

### Draft
For most of my career, I used tools built by other people.

CRM platforms.

Advertising platforms.

Analytics dashboards.

Automation systems.

Website builders.

I learned how to use them to generate leads, improve campaigns, and support business growth.

But building QueryCite has changed the way I look at software.

Once you begin creating a product, every feature becomes a business decision.

Who is this for?

What problem is important enough to solve?

What should remain simple?

What can wait?

What will a user misunderstand?

What does the product promise—and can the system actually deliver it?

I am not trying to become a software engineer.

I am learning how to work across marketing, customer problems, product decisions, AI, and technology.

That shift has made me a better marketer too.

Because marketing a product is easier when you understand why it was built, what trade-offs shaped it, and where it may fail the user.

The biggest change in my career is not that I can now help create websites or applications.

It is that I have stopped seeing marketing as the final step after a product is built.

Marketing should influence what gets built in the first place.

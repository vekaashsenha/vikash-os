## 2. What Each Stakeholder Cares About

### Travel

- Reliable booking support.
- Smooth coordination across flights, hotels, cars, and changes.
- Policy compliance without constant follow-up.
- Fast issue resolution during trips.

### Procurement

- Clear commercials.
- Negotiation room.
- Vendor stability.
- Contract terms, service levels, and measurable value.

### Finance

- Cost visibility.
- Invoice accuracy.
- Spend control.
- Reporting discipline.
- Reduced leakage and surprise expenses.

### HR

- Employee experience.
- Safety and duty of care.
- Minimal friction for frequent travellers.
- Policy adoption without employee frustration.

### Employees

- Easy booking.
- Quick response.
- Fewer last-minute surprises.
- Support when plans change.
- A feeling that the company travel process works for them, not against them.

## 3. Buying Triggers

Use this section to capture why a company starts looking for a corporate travel solution.

Possible triggers to validate:

- Travel volume increases.
- Manual coordination becomes difficult.
- Employee complaints rise.
- Costs become harder to control.
- Leadership wants better visibility.
- Existing vendor fails during critical trips.
- Policy compliance becomes inconsistent.
- Finance needs cleaner reporting.

## 4. Sources Of Hesitation

Use real examples where possible.

Potential hesitation areas:

- Fear of service disruption during transition.
- Concern that employees will resist a new process.
- Doubt about whether savings claims are realistic.
- Worry about hidden costs or weak support.
- Unclear ownership between travel, procurement, finance, and HR.
- Previous poor experience with travel vendors.

## 6. What Marketing Should Communicate

Marketing should communicate:

- Operational reliability.
- Cost visibility.
- Human support during exceptions.
- Employee experience.
- Clear reporting and governance.
- Proof of understanding corporate travel complexity.

Marketing should avoid:

- Generic promises like "seamless travel" without proof.
- Only talking about discounts.
- Ignoring finance, HR, and traveller concerns.
- Overstating technology if service execution is the real differentiator.

## 7. What Sales Should Avoid

Sales should avoid:

- Selling only to procurement and ignoring travel or HR.
- Treating price as the only buying driver.
- Making unsupported savings claims.
- Underestimating change management.
- Using generic decks that do not reflect the company's travel context.

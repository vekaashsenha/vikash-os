One sentence from my AGMP class at IIM Ahmedabad has stayed with me ever since:

A business promise is also an operations promise.

A business may promise:

Fast service.

Personalisation.

Convenience.

Quick support.

Delivery within a certain time.

Customers never experience your marketing.

They experience what happens after they buy.

If capacity is insufficient, the promise fails.

If one bottleneck slows the entire flow, the promise fails.

If customers are passed between departments and forced to wait, the promise fails.

The class helped me see operations differently.

I used to think operations was simply execution.

Now I see it differently.

Operations decides whether strategy survives contact with the customer.

Operations is the system that determines whether the strategy can actually be delivered.

The sequence is simple:

Corporate strategy defines what the business wants to promise.

Customers choose based on that promise.

Operations must then design the process, capacity, buffers, and customer flow required to fulfil it.

This made me reflect on marketing too.

Marketers often create strong promises without asking whether the business has the operational capacity to deliver them consistently.

A campaign can generate demand.

But if the process cannot absorb that demand, marketing may only expose the weakness faster.

My biggest takeaway:

Before making a customer promise, ask whether the operating system can honour it.

What is one promise your business makes that depends heavily on operations to deliver?

# Vikash Industry Intelligence Framework

Abbreviation: VIIF

## Purpose

VIIF is a structured process for understanding how different industries operate, how their business models influence buying behaviour, how ideal customers should be defined, how buying committees and personas differ, and how those insights should shape marketing, sales, customer success, and AI opportunities.

The framework exists to make industry research practical, reusable, and honest. It should help convert research into decisions, content, playbooks, and useful business thinking.

## Category Discipline

Broad labels such as "IT" or "Technology" are too generic.

Where relevant, treat these as separate categories:

- IT Services
- Cybersecurity
- Cloud and Computing
- SaaS
- Enterprise Software
- Managed Services
- Technology Consulting
- Infrastructure Services

The goal is not to make categories complicated. The goal is to avoid weak analysis caused by grouping different business models, buyers, risks, and decision processes under one broad label.

## Phase 0: Define the Purpose

- Why are we researching this industry?
- Who will use the output?
- What decision should the research support?
- Which final assets will be created?

## Phase 1: Industry Definition and Boundary

- Define exactly what is included.
- Define what is excluded.
- Identify adjacent industries.
- Avoid grouping distinct industries under broad labels.

## Phase 2: Industry Landscape

- Market maturity.
- Industry trends.
- Competitive structure.
- Regulation.
- Technology shifts.
- Customer expectations.
- Major operational challenges.
- Only use verified data when research is performed later.

## Phase 3: Business Model Analysis

- Revenue model.
- Cost structure.
- Profit drivers.
- Growth priorities.
- Commercial risks.
- Operational pressures.
- Key performance indicators.

## Phase 4: Market Segmentation

- Company size.
- Geography.
- Industry vertical.
- Growth stage.
- Ownership type.
- Technology maturity.
- Buying maturity.
- Use case.
- Spend level where relevant.

## Phase 5: Ideal Customer Profile

- Organizational profile.
- Business situation.
- Existing systems.
- Buying readiness.
- Pain intensity.
- Strategic fit.
- Disqualifying criteria.

## Phase 6: Buying Committee

For each stakeholder include:

- Role.
- Responsibility.
- KPI.
- Decision power.
- Influence level.
- Business objective.
- Risk.
- Objection.
- Evidence required.
- Preferred message.

## Phase 7: Buyer Personas

For each important stakeholder include:

- Professional context.
- Goals.
- Daily pressures.
- Pain points.
- Fears.
- Motivations.
- Objections.
- Information sources.
- Trust signals.
- Preferred communication.
- What causes rejection.

- Onboarding.
- Implementation.

- Value realization.

## Phase 14: AI Opportunities

Evaluate AI opportunities in:

- Marketing.
- Sales.
- Customer success.
- Operations.
- Analytics.
- Internal productivity.
- Product or service delivery.

## Phase 15: Vikash's Perspective

- Practical observations.
- Cross-industry comparisons.
- Lessons from experience.
- Assumptions requiring validation.
- What makes this industry different.
- Do not invent experience or claims.

## Phase 16: Content and Knowledge Outputs

Each completed industry study may become:

- Master research document.
- LinkedIn post series.
- Website article.
- GitHub knowledge file.
- Newsletter.
- Presentation.
- Workshop.
- Interview example.
- Marketing playbook.
- Sales playbook.

## Final Review Standard

Before using the research externally, confirm:

- The industry is narrowly defined.
- Facts are sourced.
- Assumptions are labelled.
- ICP refers to organizations, not individual personas.
- Buying committee roles are separated from buyer personas.
- Decision power and influence are not treated as the same thing.
- Marketing, sales, customer success, and AI recommendations come from the research.

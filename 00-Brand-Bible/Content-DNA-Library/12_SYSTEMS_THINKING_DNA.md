# Systems Thinking DNA

## Purpose
Explain how dependencies, bottlenecks, incentives, capacity, delays, and feedback loops shape an outcome that cannot be understood through one isolated action.

## Best use cases
Operational promises, marketing-to-sales flow, product-building dependencies, B2B trust cycles, service capacity, and repeated failures.

## Recommended content pillars
Marketing Beyond Marketing, AGMP With Vikash, Build With Vikash, Mobility With Vikash, and Leadership With Vikash.

## Core narrative structure
Visible outcome -> connected components -> constraint or feedback loop -> system consequence -> leverage point.

## Hook patterns
- "A small mistake in one part can stop everything."
- "The campaign created demand. The process still had to absorb it."

## Body development
Trace causes across the process. Explain reinforcing or balancing feedback where relevant, and distinguish individual action from system design.

## Ending patterns
End with the leverage point, changed process question, or system condition that matters next.

## Tone
Analytical, plain-spoken, fair, and practical.

## Evidence requirements
Dependencies and causal claims must be supported by the incident, process evidence, or clearly labelled hypothesis.

## What to avoid
Blaming an individual where system design caused the behaviour, drawing elaborate diagrams without evidence, and confusing correlation with a feedback loop.

## Example topics based on genuine experience
- Why a marketing promise depends on process, capacity, buffers, and customer flow.
- How one missing folder, permission, or integration stopped a build workflow.
- Why B2B trust and results develop through a wider system over time.

## Quality checklist
- [ ] The system boundary is clear.
- [ ] Dependencies and delays are supported.
- [ ] Individual blame is not substituted for system analysis.
- [ ] A practical leverage point is identified.

## Strong combinations with other DNA
Business Diagnosis for underlying causes; AI Builder for build systems; Marketing Myth for oversimplified beliefs.

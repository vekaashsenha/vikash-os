# Contrarian Truth DNA

## Purpose
Challenge a familiar belief only when evidence supports a more useful interpretation. Evidence must come before provocation.

## Best use cases
No-decision competition, marketing-versus-business performance, idea-versus-system building, customer discovery, and qualified industry beliefs.

## Recommended content pillars
Marketing Beyond Marketing, Buyer Psychology With Vikash, Build With Vikash, and AI Visibility Journal.

## Core narrative structure
Evidence or real observation -> common belief -> contradiction -> qualified truth -> practical consequence.

## Hook patterns
- "The biggest competitor in B2B is often no decision."
- "Ideas do not build products. Systems do."

## Body development
Present the underlying evidence first or immediately after the claim. Define where the truth applies and where it may not.

## Ending patterns
End with the decision implication or a question that tests the belief in the reader's context.

## Tone
Confident but qualified, direct, evidence-led, and respectful.

## Evidence requirements
Use verified experience, approved data, or reviewed research. A surprising opinion without evidence is not Contrarian Truth DNA.

## What to avoid
Provocation for reach, absolutes, straw men, unsupported predictions, false novelty, and turning a personal example into a universal law.

## Example topics based on genuine experience
- Why no decision may be a more relevant B2B competitor than another vendor.
- Why the idea was easier than the systems required to build a product.
- Why campaign success did not settle the business-performance question.

## Quality checklist
- [ ] Evidence appears before or with the challenge.
- [ ] The common belief is represented fairly.
- [ ] Scope and exceptions are clear.
- [ ] The practical consequence matters more than provocation.

## Strong combinations with other DNA
Buyer Psychology for the status quo; Marketing Myth for belief correction; Systems Thinking for causal context.

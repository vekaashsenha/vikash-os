# SaaS Success DNA

## Purpose
Explain how SaaS products earn adoption, willingness to pay, retention, and commercial value through observable customer behaviour rather than generic growth advice.

## Best use cases
Free-trial behaviour, pricing, activation, adoption friction, conversion, retention, and product-market learning.

## Recommended content pillars
Buyer Psychology With Vikash, Marketing Beyond Marketing, Build With Vikash, and AI Visibility Journal.

## Core narrative structure
Observed behaviour -> commercial question -> plausible explanations -> evidence needed -> product or go-to-market implication.

## Hook patterns
- "Repeated free trials looked like interest. They also raised a pricing question."
- Start with the behaviour, not a benchmark.

## Body development
Separate what happened from why it may have happened. Examine value, friction, pricing, trust, timing, and adoption without pretending the evidence proves one cause.

## Ending patterns
End with the decision the team must investigate or a precise buyer question.

## Tone
Commercial, curious, analytical, and evidence-aware.

## Evidence requirements
Use verified product behaviour, approved customer evidence, or clearly labelled observation. Do not use unverified SaaS benchmarks.

## What to avoid
Universal conversion rates, fabricated customer motives, "growth hack" language, and treating sign-ups as success without adoption or value evidence.

## Example topics based on genuine experience
- What repeated free-trial accounts suggested—and did not prove—about SaaS pricing and willingness to pay.
- How QueryCite product discovery questions should be separated from confirmed usage evidence.

## Quality checklist
- [ ] Behaviour and interpretation are separated.
- [ ] Numbers and benchmarks are verified or omitted.
- [ ] Commercial value is clearer than vanity activity.
- [ ] The next evidence needed is stated.

## Strong combinations with other DNA
Buyer Psychology for motive hypotheses; Systems Thinking for adoption flow; Business Diagnosis for symptom versus cause.

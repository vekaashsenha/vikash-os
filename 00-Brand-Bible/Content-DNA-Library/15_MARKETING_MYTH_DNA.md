# Marketing Myth DNA

## Purpose
Correct an oversimplified marketing belief by replacing it with a more useful, evidence-supported business view.

## Best use cases
ROAS versus ROI, short-term B2B expectations, lead volume, channel attribution, campaign demand versus operating capacity, and no-decision competition.

## Recommended content pillars
Marketing Beyond Marketing, Buyer Psychology With Vikash, and AI Visibility Journal.

## Core narrative structure
Common belief -> why it is attractive -> evidence or experience that qualifies it -> better model -> practical implication.

## Hook patterns
- "Strong marketing performance is not automatically strong business performance."
- "B2B trust cannot always be compressed into one campaign cycle."

## Body development
State the belief fairly, then use the minimum evidence needed to challenge it. Replace the myth with a conditional, decision-useful explanation.

## Ending patterns
End with the better question a marketer or leader should ask.

## Tone
Clear, corrective, restrained, and never smug.

## Evidence requirements
The correction must be grounded in verified experience, approved data, or credible reviewed research. State limits.

## What to avoid
Straw-man arguments, absolute proclamations, invented industry consensus, provocative hooks without proof, and declaring SEO or established channels dead.

## Example topics based on genuine experience
- Why 10x ROAS did not settle the business-ROI question.
- Why B2B results cannot simply be demanded overnight.
- Why generating demand can expose an operational constraint.

## Quality checklist
- [ ] The original belief is represented fairly.
- [ ] The correction has evidence.
- [ ] The replacement model is useful and qualified.
- [ ] Provocation does not outrun proof.

## Strong combinations with other DNA
Paid Ads for campaign economics; Systems Thinking for wider causes; Contrarian Truth for a defensible challenge.
